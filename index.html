The 2026 Boston University Graphic Design MFA Exhibition takes its title from a file extension. Hyper.txt evokes the language of early computing: plain text, unstyled, foundational. At the same time, it gestures toward hypertext and the logic of linking, where one idea opens onto another and meaning unfolds through connection rather than sequence. By pairing “hyper” with “.txt,” this exhibition proposes a productive tension: “hyper” suggests acceleration and intensity, while .txt conjures simplicity and directness.

The twenty-three Graphic Design MFA candidates whose work is featured in Hyper.txt design within a networked field. Their projects do not operate as isolated artifacts but as nodes within larger systems of research, material exploration, and cultural inquiry. Posters, books, installations, and experiments move between concept and form, bridging personal investigation and shared discourse. Ideas branch outward; methodologies intersect; visual languages cross-reference one another.

The term “hyper” suggests velocity and excess, but also attention. To be hyper-aware is to notice relationships that might otherwise remain latent. For this year’s cohort of designers, “hyper” signals a willingness to heighten and intensify visual language—while remaining grounded in rigorous research and craft. The projects on view range from large-scale visual systems to printed matter, from conceptual investigations to materially driven experiments.

Hyper.txt also acknowledges the contemporary condition of reading and making. We navigate through screens, archives, feeds, and databases; we move laterally as often as we move forward. The designers in this cohort engage critically with that condition. Some question the aesthetics of interface and authorship; others explore typography, translation, identity, and cultural memory. Many move fluidly between print and digital spaces, testing where one ends and another begins. Their work resists singular definition, embracing instead a porous and evolving understanding of graphic design.

If a hyperlink invites us elsewhere, a .txt file asks us to slow down and attend to what is directly in front of us. Hyper.txt holds both impulses. It is an invitation to move—across ideas, across media, across contexts—while remaining attentive to the larger field of graphic design.


Kristen Coogan
Associate Professor of Art, Graphic Design
